CHAPTER 7: THE HUNT

Earth - New York City
Two Months After Dreius's Message

The USOF safe house was in Brooklyn, tucked between a laundromat and a bodega, unremarkable in every way. Which was exactly the point.

Agent Maria Torres of the FBI's Joint Alien Affairs Division had been watching it for three weeks. She'd seen twelve different people enter and leave. She'd logged their movements, their patterns, their routines. And tonight, based on intelligence from Agent Seraph, tonight was when they'd finally have enough evidence to move.

"All units, stand by," she whispered into her comm. "Target is approaching from the north."

Through her scope, she watched a man in his forties, carrying a briefcase, walk casually down the street. According to Assonian intelligence, the briefcase contained fifty thousand dollars in cash—payment for explosives that would be used to bomb the Assonian Embassy in Geneva. Again.

The man entered the safe house.

"Alpha team, green light," Torres said. "Execute."

Four blocks away, Dr. Sarah Chen sat in a mobile command center with Agent Seraph, watching through surveillance feeds as FBI tactical teams converged on the building. This was the seventh USOF cell they'd hit in the past month. Each one had brought them closer to the leadership, to the corporate money, to the people orchestrating the entire conspiracy.

"Breaching now," the tactical commander's voice came through the speakers.

On the screens, they watched as agents in tactical gear smashed through doors, moved with practiced precision through rooms. Shouts. Movement. Then—

"All targets secured. No shots fired. Repeat, no shots fired. We have twelve suspects in custody and... Jesus Christ, we have a lot of hardware here."

The camera feeds showed what he meant. The safe house wasn't just a meeting place. It was an armory. Explosives, automatic weapons, even what looked like military-grade plasma rifles—Assonian technology, stolen or bought on black markets.

"Bag everything," Chen ordered. "I want serial numbers, manufacturers, every piece of evidence we can trace."

"Copy that, Doctor."

Seraph leaned back in her chair, studying the suspects being led out in handcuffs. "That's cell seven. We have five more confirmed locations and possibly three we're still tracking."

"How long before they realize we're systematically dismantling their entire network?" Chen asked.

"They already know," Seraph said. "The question is whether they accelerate their timeline or go deeper underground. Based on the chatter we've been monitoring, I think they're panicking. Which makes them more dangerous."

Torres's voice came through: "We've got something. One of the suspects is asking for a deal. Says he has information about the leadership. About who's really funding this."

Chen and Seraph exchanged glances.

"Bring him to the command center," Chen said. "Let's hear what he has to say."

Twenty Minutes Later

The man sat across from Chen and Seraph, his hands still cuffed, his lawyer beside him. He was thin, nervous, sweating despite the cool temperature.

"My client," the lawyer began, "is willing to provide substantial information about USOF leadership and operations in exchange for immunity and witness protection."

"Let's hear what he's offering first," Chen said. "Then we'll discuss deals."

The man—his name was David Reese, according to his file—licked his lips. "I can give you names. Real names. The people funding us, coordinating operations, planning the attacks. But I need guarantees. These people... they have reach. They can get to me anywhere."

"Including in an Assonian facility?" Seraph asked quietly.

Reese looked at her, seeing the alien features, the calm competence. "You're one of them. One of the aliens."

"I'm Assonian, yes. And I'm offering you protection that no human organization could match. Our facilities are impenetrable. Our security is absolute. If you cooperate, we can ensure your safety in ways your government cannot."

"She's right," Chen added. "The USOF has penetrated some law enforcement agencies, some political offices. But they haven't penetrated Assonian security. You'd be safer with them than with us."

Reese was quiet for a moment, then: "Okay. Okay, I'll talk. But you need to understand, these people... they're not just terrorists. They're billionaires. CEOs. People with private armies and government connections. They've been planning this for years, since before the Assonians even revealed themselves."

"Planning what?" Chen asked.

"The long war," Reese said. "They don't want quick victory or quick defeat. They want perpetual conflict. Earth and Asson locked in endless fighting, both sides bleeding resources, neither able to win decisively. Because that's when they make their real money. Selling weapons to both sides, reconstruction contracts, security services, everything."

He pulled out a data chip—somehow he'd managed to keep it hidden even through the arrest.

"This has everything. Names, account numbers, communication logs. The plan isn't just to break the treaty. It's to ensure that Earth and Asson stay enemies for generations. And they're using that exiled Assonian lord—Dreius—as the trigger. When he attacks, when the violence starts, they profit from both sides' fear."

Seraph took the data chip, her expression unreadable. "How did you get this?"

"I'm an accountant," Reese said. "Was an accountant. They hired me to manage some of the financial transfers, keep things clean, make sure the money couldn't be traced back to the corporations. But I kept copies. Insurance, you know? In case they decided I was a loose end."

"Smart," Chen said. "And you're willing to testify to all of this?"

"If you keep me alive, yeah." Reese looked at both of them. "Look, I didn't sign up for this believing in their cause. I did it for the money. But when I saw what they were really planning, how many people would die just so they could get richer..." He shook his head. "I'm not a good person. But I'm not that bad. Give me immunity and protection, and I'll burn them all down for you."

Chen looked at Seraph, who nodded slightly.

"Deal," Chen said. "Agent Torres, process Mr. Reese's transfer to Assonian custody. Agent Seraph will handle his protection personally."

As Torres led Reese away, Chen plugged the data chip into her terminal. What came up made her blood run cold.

Names. Dozens of them. CEOs of major defense contractors. Executives from resource extraction companies. Even two sitting members of Congress.

And financial records showing a network that spread across fifty countries, managing billions of dollars, all coordinated to ensure that Earth and Asson remained enemies.

"This is going to cause an earthquake," Chen said quietly.

"Good," Seraph replied. "Let it. These people tried to engineer a war between our civilizations. They deserve whatever earthquake is coming."

Courosaunt - Chancellor's Office
Same Day

Thiurate read the intelligence report with growing horror. The scope of the conspiracy was staggering. The USOF wasn't just a terrorist organization—it was a corporate-backed operation involving some of the wealthiest and most powerful people on Earth.

"How do we proceed?" he asked, looking at his father and General Arcan.

"Carefully," Keeson said. "If we simply release this information, it'll look like we're trying to destabilize Earth's governments. We hand them ammunition to claim we're interfering in human affairs."

"But if we don't release it," Arcan countered, "we're protecting the people trying to kill us. Chancellor, these corporations have been funding attacks on our facilities, on our people. They're accomplices to terrorism."

"Which is why Earth needs to be the one to expose them," Thiurate said. He activated his comm. "Dr. Chen, you're seeing the same data I am?"

"Yes, Chancellor," Chen's voice came through. "And I'm already coordinating with President Vasquez. We're preparing a joint announcement. Earth will lead on the human conspirators, Asson will provide the evidence. That way it's clear we're not interfering—we're assisting Earth's investigation of its own criminals."

"When?" Thiurate asked.

"Forty-eight hours. We need that time to coordinate arrests, secure assets, prepare for the fallout. Chancellor, when this goes public, it's going to shake both our civilizations. People will demand accountability. Some will claim the evidence is fabricated. It'll be messy."

"Messy is better than the alternative," Thiurate said. "Forty-eight hours. We'll be ready."

He ended the call and turned to face his father and Arcan.

"When this breaks, the USOF is finished," Thiurate said. "But that doesn't mean we're safe. Cornered enemies are dangerous, and these people have resources. General, I want maximum security on all our facilities for the next seventy-two hours. Assume they'll try something desperate."

"Already on it, Chancellor," Arcan said. "But there's something else we need to discuss. Dreius."

Thiurate felt his stomach tighten. "What about him?"

"He's been quiet since he sent that message two months ago. No attacks, no movements, just building his infrastructure on Kistin. But our long-range sensors have picked up unusual activity in his sector over the last week. Multiple ships arriving and departing, weapons testing, mobilization patterns."

"You think he's preparing to attack despite his message?" Thiurate asked.

"I think he's preparing for something," Arcan said. "Whether it's an attack on us, on the USOF, or something else entirely, I can't say. But he's not sitting idle."

Keeson spoke up. "Has he responded to any of our subsequent communications?"

"No," Arcan admitted. "After that first message through Seraph, nothing. We've tried to establish dialogue, but he's gone silent."

"Then we watch and wait," Thiurate decided. "If he attacks, we defend. But I'm not going to assume hostility just because he's mobilizing. He told us the USOF was now his enemy. Maybe he's preparing to deal with them in his own way."

"And if his way involves civilian casualties?" Arcan pressed.

"Then we stop him," Thiurate said firmly. "But we don't stop him preemptively. We're not his enemies, General. We're his alternative. Let's make sure we act like it."

Kistin - Dreius's Command Center
Same Time

Lord Dreius stood before a holographic display showing USOF cell locations across Earth and several other worlds. His intelligence network—built from scratch over the past six months—had been tracking them just as Asson's had.

"Confirmed targets," he said to his assembled officers. "Seventeen USOF cells, eight corporate headquarters involved in funding, and one central coordination facility. All of them have been planning attacks on both Asson and Earth interests."

"My Lord," Commander Valerius said carefully, "are we really going to strike human targets? That would be seen as an act of war, regardless of whether the targets are legitimate."

"We're not striking Earth," Dreius corrected. "We're striking USOF facilities. There's a difference. And we're going to make that difference very clear."

He pulled up another display, showing a coordinated assault plan.

"In forty-eight hours, my brother is going to expose the USOF conspiracy. Earth authorities will move to arrest the leadership, seize assets, dismantle the organization. It'll be a joint Earth-Asson operation, very public, very political." He smiled slightly. "And in all that chaos, no one will notice that several key USOF facilities simply... disappear. Quietly. Permanently."

"You want to hit them while Earth authorities are making arrests?" one of his officers asked.

"I want to ensure that the USOF is destroyed completely," Dreius said. "My brother's approach will get the leadership, the public figures. But the USOF isn't just its public face. It has hidden facilities, backup plans, resources stashed in places Earth authorities will never find. I'm going to find them. And I'm going to eliminate them."

He turned to face his officers.

"Here's what you need to understand. The USOF tried to use me to start a war. They thought I was a blunt instrument, a useful fool who'd attack my brother and create the chaos they wanted. They were wrong. And now they're going to learn what happens when you try to manipulate someone who's better at this game than you are."

"But Lord Dreius," Valerius said, "won't this look like you're cooperating with Asson? With the treaty?"

"It'll look like I'm protecting my own interests," Dreius corrected. "The USOF was funding me with the expectation that I'd follow their plan. I took their money and built my forces, but I never agreed to be their puppet. This is me settling that account. Violently."

He pulled up specific target locations.

"These facilities are off Earth, in neutral territory or failed colonies. No civilian casualties, no Earth sovereignty violations. We hit them hard, we hit them fast, and we make sure there's nothing left for them to rebuild with. By the time my brother's public arrests happen, the USOF won't have any hidden resources to fall back on. They'll be truly finished."

"And then what, my Lord?" an officer asked. "After the USOF is destroyed, what's our relationship with Asson?"

Dreius was quiet for a moment.

"Then we continue building what we're building here. We prove that order and strength create better societies than compromise and cooperation. We demonstrate that my path works. And eventually, when Asson sees what we've accomplished, when they see the difference between their approach and mine..." He smiled. "Then they'll have to make a choice. And I'm confident about what they'll choose."

"You still believe you'll win," Valerius said, not quite a question.

"I believe truth wins," Dreius said. "And the truth is that civilizations built on clear hierarchies, on meritocracy, on the understanding that some people are better suited to lead than others—those civilizations survive. Civilizations built on endless consensus-seeking and compromise? They stagnate and fail. I'm going to prove that. Just not the way the USOF wanted."

He turned back to the display.

"Prepare the strike teams. We launch in forty-six hours, two hours before my brother's public announcement. I want every USOF hidden facility destroyed before they even know we're coming."

"Yes, my Lord."

As his officers dispersed to prepare, Valerius lingered.

"My Lord, may I speak freely?"

"Always."

"You're helping your brother, even though you're still his ideological enemy. Even though you still believe he's wrong."

"Yes," Dreius said simply.

"Why?"

Dreius looked at the display showing both Asson's planned arrests and his own planned strikes. Two brothers, working toward the same goal from opposite directions, neither willing to admit they might need each other.

"Because," Dreius said quietly, "my brother may be naive about how to build a civilization. But he was right about one thing: the USOF represents something worse than either of our philosophies. They don't believe in order or cooperation. They believe in profit through chaos. And that's something neither of us can tolerate."

He paused, then continued.

"I still think he's wrong about how to lead. I still think his treaty will fail, that his approach will cost lives, that eventually Asson will need the kind of decisive leadership I represent. But I'd rather prove that through success than through someone else's manipulation. When I defeat my brother's philosophy, I want it to be clean. Honest. A genuine test of whose approach works better."

"That's remarkably principled for someone who claims principles are a luxury," Valerius said with a slight smile.

"Principles aren't a luxury," Dreius corrected. "Naive idealism is. There's a difference. I have principles. I believe in order, in strength, in meritocracy. Those principles are why I oppose my brother—and why I oppose the USOF. Both threaten the kind of civilization I'm trying to build, just in different ways."

He looked at his second-in-command.

"In forty-six hours, we're going to prove that strength and order can be wielded against chaos rather than cooperation. We're going to show the galaxy that my way works. And we're going to do it without becoming the monster my brother thinks I am."

Valerius nodded slowly. "Understood, my Lord. I'll prepare the teams."

As Valerius left, Dreius stood alone, looking at the holographic display of two coordinated operations—one public, one hidden—that would destroy the USOF completely.

His brother thought he was a warmonger.

The USOF thought he was a useful idiot.

Neither understood that Dreius, son of Keeson, was playing a longer game than anyone realized.

And in forty-six hours, the entire galaxy would see it.

Earth - Washington D.C.
President's Office - 24 Hours Before the Announcement

President Elena Vasquez sat in her office, reviewing the arrest warrants. Fifty-three of them. CEOs, executives, politicians. People she'd worked with, shaken hands with, attended fundraisers with. People who'd been engineering a war while smiling to her face.

"We're ready, Madam President," her chief of staff said. "All agencies coordinated. The arrests will happen simultaneously at 0600 tomorrow. The evidence is airtight."

"And the public reaction?" Vasquez asked.

"Will be chaos," her chief of staff admitted. "Some will call this a witch hunt, claim the Assonians fabricated evidence. Others will demand even more arrests, claim the corruption goes deeper. It'll be the biggest political scandal in history."

"Good," Vasquez said. "These people tried to start a war that would have killed billions. I want chaos. I want outrage. I want everyone to understand exactly what was almost done to us."

She signed the last warrant.

"Execute the operation. And God help us all with what comes next."

Kistin - Dreius's Flagship - 2 Hours Before Asson's Announcement

"All strike teams are in position, my Lord," Valerius reported. "Seventeen targets confirmed. We're ready on your command."

Dreius looked at the tactical display. His forces were positioned at seventeen locations across former Lumina space—every hidden USOF facility his intelligence network had identified. Small teams, surgical strikes, designed to eliminate infrastructure without causing broader conflict.

"Execute," he said simply.

Across a dozen worlds, Dreius's forces moved simultaneously. USOF bunkers exploded. Hidden weapons caches were destroyed. Communications facilities went dark. In less than an hour, every hidden resource the USOF had stashed for rebuilding after their public leadership was arrested simply ceased to exist.

"All targets destroyed, my Lord," Valerius reported. "Zero civilian casualties. No Assonian casualties. Mission complete."

"Good," Dreius said. "Now we wait and see what my brother does with his part."

Earth - 0600 Hours

The arrests happened simultaneously across six continents. FBI, Interpol, and local authorities, coordinated with Assonian intelligence, moved on fifty-three targets. By 0615, all were in custody.

By 0700, President Vasquez and Chancellor Thiurate appeared together on a joint broadcast, explaining everything. The conspiracy, the evidence, the arrests. The corporate backing, the profit motive, the attempt to engineer perpetual war.

The reaction was immediate and explosive.

Courosaunt - Chancellor's Office - 12 Hours After the Announcement

Thiurate watched the Earth news feeds with exhaustion. Protests, counter-protests, political resignations, emergency legislative sessions. Exactly the chaos his father had predicted.

But it was productive chaos. The kind that came from exposing corruption rather than hiding it.

"Chancellor," Agent Seraph entered his office. "We have a problem. Or maybe an opportunity, I'm not sure which."

"What is it?"

"Seventeen USOF facilities in neutral space were destroyed last night. All of them hidden locations we hadn't identified yet. All destroyed with military precision, zero collateral damage."

Thiurate felt his pulse quicken. "Dreius."

"Almost certainly," Seraph confirmed. "He hit them two hours before our public announcement, while they were still scrambling to figure out what we knew. Wiped out their backup plans completely. The USOF is now truly finished—no hidden resources, no fallback positions, nothing."

"He helped us," Thiurate said quietly. "Without coordination, without asking for credit, he helped destroy the USOF."

"While also demonstrating his military capability and intelligence network," Seraph added. "Chancellor, your brother just proved he could hit targets across multiple star systems simultaneously with perfect precision. That's both helpful and terrifying."

Thiurate stood, moving to the window. Out there, somewhere in the ruins of old empires, his brother was building an alternative civilization. One based on strength and order rather than cooperation and compromise.

And despite being ideological enemies, despite still believing the other was fundamentally wrong, they'd just worked together to destroy a common enemy.

"Send him a message," Thiurate said.

"What should it say, Chancellor?"

Thiurate thought for a long moment.

"Tell him... tell him thank you. Tell him his assistance was noted and appreciated. And tell him that when he's ready to discuss what happens next between us, I'm willing to talk. Not as enemies. As brothers who disagree but don't have to destroy each other over it."

Seraph nodded. "I'll send it. Chancellor, do you think he'll respond?"

"I don't know," Thiurate admitted. "But I hope so. Because I'm starting to think that maybe—just maybe—the galaxy doesn't have to choose between his way and mine. Maybe there's room for both."

"That's very optimistic, Chancellor."

"I know," Thiurate said. "My father would be proud."

Kistin - Three Days Later

Dreius read Thiurate's message three times.

Thank you. Appreciated. Ready to talk.

Simple words. But they carried weight.

His brother was offering dialogue. Not surrender, not alliance, but genuine conversation between equals who respected each other despite fundamental disagreement.

Dreius could ignore it. Continue building his empire in isolation, preparing for the eventual confrontation when he'd prove his philosophy superior.

Or he could do something unexpected.

He began typing a response:

Brother,

I don't regret what I did to the USOF. They tried to use me, and I don't allow anyone to use me. This wasn't cooperation with you—it was protecting my own interests. Our goals happened to align. This time.

But I accept your offer of dialogue. Not because I think you'll convince me I'm wrong. Not because I'm ready to rejoin Asson or support your treaty. But because I'm curious what you think happens next.

You have your way. I have mine. Both are being tested. Your peaceful cooperation with Earth, my orderly meritocracy on Kistin. Let's see which one succeeds in building something worth defending.

I'll meet with you. Neutral territory. No guards, no weapons, just us. Two brothers talking about how to build civilizations in a universe that seems determined to choose violence over everything else.

Let me know when and where.

- Dreius

He sent the message, then sat back.

His father would have been proud of Thiurate for offering dialogue.

His father would have been disappointed in him for accepting it instead of pursuing total victory.

But Dreius wasn't his father. He was something different. Something new.

And maybe, just maybe, his brother was too.

Time would tell which of them was building the future.

But at least they'd both survived to see it tested.
